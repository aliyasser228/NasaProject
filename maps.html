OZONE

Enavi ozone is on track, helping to avoid global warming and reaching 0.5°C

Facts and figures about ozone protection include:

  • Treaties: The 1985 Vienna Convention and the 1987 Montreal Protocol were established to protect the ozone layer. Both treaties have achieved universal ratification by 198 parties.
  • Montreal Protocol: It controls nearly 100 ozone-depleting substances (ODSs), most of which are also potent greenhouse gases. The Kigali Amendment added 18 hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) compounds to the list of controlled substances.
  • Climate Benefits: Ozone protection efforts have significantly contributed to slowing climate change by avoiding emissions estimated at 135 billion tons of CO2 equivalent from 1990 to 2010.
  • Recovery: Recent studies indicate the recovery of the ozone layer and the reduction in the size and depth of the ozone hole over the Antarctic since 2000. The ozone layer is expected to return to 1980 values by 2066 in the Antarctic, 2045 in the Arctic, and 2040 in the mid-latitudes (60°N - 60°S).

Ozone treaties and SDGs

The ozone layer protects people and life on Earth. The Ozone Secretariat works with all nations to protect the ozone layer. Successfully protecting the ozone layer help deliver many of the Sustainable Development Goals.

The Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer

The Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer is a global agreement to protect the Earth’s ozone layer by phasing out the chemicals that deplete it. This phase-out plan includes both the production and consumption of ozone-depleting substances. The landmark agreement was signed in 1987 and entered into force in 1989. The parties to the Protocol meet once a year to make decisions aimed at ensuring the successful implementation of the agreement. These include adjusting or amending the Protocol, which has been done six times since its creation. The most recent amendment, the Kigali Amendment, called for the phase-down of hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) in 2016. These HFCs were used as replacements for a batch of ozone-depleting substances eliminated by the original Montreal Protocol. Although they do not deplete the ozone layer, they are known to be powerful greenhouse gases and, thus, contributors to climate change. The Montreal Protocol provided a set of practical, actionable tasks that were universally agreed on. The Protocol has successfully met its objectives thus far and continues to safeguard the ozone layer today. Thanks to the collaborative effort of nations around the world, the ozone layer is well on its way to recovery.
